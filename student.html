Nebula [noun]:

An interstellar cloud of dust, hydrogen, helium and other

ionized gases. Originally, the term was used to describe any

diffused astronomical object, including galaxies

beyond the Milky Way.

Emission Nebulae

An emission nebula is a cloud of hot gas. Inside these, a star energizes the atoms in the cloud with ultraviolet radiation. As these atoms' energy lowers, they give off radiation. This causes the nebula to glow. Emission Nebulae tend to be red in color because there's a lot of hydrogen. Additional colors, such as blue and green, can be produced by the atoms of other elements.

Reflection Nebulae

A reflection nebula differs from an emission nebula in that it does not produce its own radiation. It is a dust and gas cloud that reflects the energy of light from a star or group of stars nearby. The sites of star formation are often Reflection Nebulae. Because of the way that the light is dispersed, they typically appear to be blue in colour. Blue light is more efficiently scattered.

Dark Nebulae

A dark nebula is a dust cloud that hides the light behind it from objects. In structure, they are somewhat similar to reflection nebulae and appear distinct largely because of the light source placement. Dark nebulae are often seen with emission and reflection nebulae.

Planetary Nebulae

 As it nears the end of its life cycle, a star forms a shell of gas known as a planetary nebula. It can be a tad misleading to name them. They have nothing to do with the planets, really. This name was given to these nebulae because, according to their circular appearance, they also look like planets. The outer shell of gas is normally illuminated at its core by the remains of the star.

Supernova Remnants

When a star ends its existence in a massive explosion known as a supernova, supernova remnants are formed. The blast throws a massive volume of matter from the star into space. With the remains of the star which formed it, this cloud of matter glows.
